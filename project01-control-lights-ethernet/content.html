0. Intro

Controlling lights with an Arduino is probably one of the first things that come into our minds when we think about automating our home or office. It's relatively simple to integrate into the existing legacy electrical system. I.e. there is no need to install actuators, motors, valves, sensors, etc. Only a nice relay would do a pretty good job.

1. Utility

#1. Switch lights on/off without leaving the comfortable position at desk

From our experience, the most interesting use case is at our office. We work in a building with several levels, each level being an open space. During the day, there is no need for artificial light. But as the sun goes down, somebody needs to physically go to the light switch to turn the lights on. This is a physically demanding task, especially after a whole day of intense workout (i.e. fingers continuously hitting keyboard/mouse).

So now, there is no need to leave the comfortable position at the computer. Everybody can control the lights from a nice web interface, within the browser (desktop, tablet, mobile).

#2. For fun: annoy colleagues by turning off lights

A corollary of the above is that now everybody can switch lights on/off. E.g. for the colleagues that work at the level below. However, this works optimally from the mobile, while the author of the prank is visiting the neighbours downstairs (in order to enjoy their reaction).

#3. Verify if we forgot (or not) the lights on when we left home

4. Working On Details

We drill holes and cut the case (for the Arduino/shield connectors). We use 5 mm nuts (with plastic washers) to attach the boards to the case. We prefer this method instead of gluing the parts with the hot glue gun.
We add the Arduino board and the relay board. For the Arduino board, although we have drilled 4 holes, we use only 3 nuts. This is because the 4th hole of the Arduino board is very close to a soldered component. For the relay board, we have used only 2 nuts. But we should have used 4, because when screwing the wires (high voltage), we need to apply pressure, and the board bends.
We add the Ethernet shield, and we connect it to the relay board.
We connect some wires to the existing light switches.
WARNING: high voltage! Always turn off power (e.g. from the fuse box of the room/building) when manipulating high voltage circuits. And this includes the relay board as well!
And then we mount the push buttons and cable ducts.
We attach the case to the wall, not far from a power outlet. In our case, we had one quite close, that powers a network switch.
We power the circuit and we test it (from the phone this time).
We fix the case cover, and we put one of the stickers from the Arduino box :).
And... voila! We are quite pleased with the visuals of the result. After the "beta" phase, we could hide (in the cable duct) the 2 wires as well: the USB cable and the network cable.
